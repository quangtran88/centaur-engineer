The reading library

Every reference from the curriculum, verified and annotated — grouped by module. Written for any engineer who works with AI coding agents and wants to be the stronger half of the pair. Back to the dashboard; the full guide is at curriculum/guide.md.

Core shelf · read in order

The six books

  • Working Backwards — Bryar & Carr workingbackwards.comThe PR/FAQ discipline: write the press release and hostile FAQ before building. Module 1's backbone.
  • The Staff Engineer's Path — Tanya Reilly · O'Reilly pageThe operating manual for the staff+ role this curriculum points at: scope, influence, and time allocation past senior.
  • Designing Data-Intensive Applications, 2nd ed. — Kleppmann · O'Reilly pageThe theory beneath your workflow engine, your partitioned database, and every consistency decision you make. Ch. 6–10 for Module 3 (2nd-ed numbering).
  • A Philosophy of Software Design, 2nd ed. — Ousterhout · official pageTaste, made teachable: deep modules, information hiding, complexity as the enemy.
  • The Mom Test — Fitzpatrick · momtestbook.comHow to extract truth from stakeholders who politely lie. Three-hour read, career-long payoff.
  • Thinking in Systems — Donella MeadowsFeedback loops, stocks, and leverage points — you'll recognize your own tooling stack and your org in it.

Module 08 · 12 weeks

Systems mechanics: the decision layer

  • DDIA 2nd ed., ch. 6–10Already on the Module 03 shelf (m3-l1/l7) — Module 08 is the applied veto-vocabulary layer on top of that reading, not a second purchase of it.
  • Consistency Models JepsenThe clickable map of the consistency ladder — every named guarantee, what implies what (m8-l1).
  • How to do distributed locking Martin KleppmannThe canonical "Redis lock" veto: GC-pause lease expiry, fencing tokens, why Redlock can't be fixed. Read antirez's reply ("Is Redlock safe?") for the counter-argument (m8-l2).
  • Raft — In Search of an Understandable Consensus Algorithm Ongaro & OusterhoutPaper + interactive visualization. Majority math, term numbers, why 2-node HA splits brain (m8-l3).
  • Saga pattern Chris RichardsonCompensation design and the pivot transaction; pair with Stripe's "Idempotent requests" API docs for the key-design half (m8-l4).
  • Use The Index, Luke Markus WinandIndex anatomy for developers: composite column order, index-only scans, and why the ORM's query defeats your index (m8-l5).
  • PostgreSQL manual — ch. 13 (locking), ch. 25 (routine vacuuming), CREATE INDEX / ALTER TABLE pages postgresql.orgThe primary source for lock-queue inversion, CONCURRENTLY / NOT VALID sequences, autovacuum and wraparound (m8-l5/l6).
  • Helland — "Life beyond Distributed Transactions" (CIDR 2007)The aggregate-is-the-atomicity-boundary argument — the upstream decider behind every SQL-vs-NoSQL debate (m8-l7). Pair with the DynamoDB USENIX ATC '22 paper for the partition half; the HNSW paper covers the vector half (m8-l12 — note pgvector implements HNSW and IVFFlat, not IVF-PQ).
  • "Scaling Memcache at Facebook" (NSDI 2013) + "Metastable Failures in Distributed Systems" (HotOS 2021)The two papers behind m8-l8/l9: leases against the stale-set race; bistable feedback loops and why remediation amplifies (m8-l8, m8-l9).
  • AWS Builders' Library Brooker, MacCárthaigh et al."Timeouts, retries, and backoff with jitter", "Avoiding overload", cell-based architecture, static stability, shuffle sharding — the practitioner half of failure physics (m8-l9/l10).
  • Google SRE book — ch. 21 Handling Overload (+ ch. 22 Cascading Failures) sre.googleRetry budgets (~10% rule), client-side throttling, load shedding — the server-side of overload control (m8-l9).
  • Jeff Dean's latency numbers (LADIS 2009) + Colin Scott's interactive updateThe napkin-math table for m8-d1 — hold the orders of magnitude in working memory and most premature migrations veto themselves.
